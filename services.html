Therapy Utrecht offers individual therapy to adults and teens, couple therapy, and a variety of workshops.

The approach of our therapists is integrative and combines a broad range of psychological and evidence-based approaches such as CBT, EMDR, Psychodynamic therapy, IFS, ACT, Schema, EFT, Somatic approaches, Gestalt therapy, etc.

  • CBT is a structured, evidence-based psychotherapy that helps clients identify and change unhelpful patterns of thinking and behavior. By learning to recognize cognitive distortions and testing them through behavioral experiments, clients develop more balanced thoughts, reduce emotional distress, and build practical skills for coping and problem-solving. CBT is effective for anxiety, depression, PTSD, OCD and many other conditions and is often time-limited and goal-focused.
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) is a therapeutic approach based on the idea that many psychological complaints stem from unresolved distressful or traumatic experiences. The theory behind EMDR suggests that these experiences can get "stuck" in the brain, causing emotional and psychological distress. EMDR is effective in treating various conditions, including PTSD, anxiety, depression, phobias, and grief. It can also benefit individuals who suffer from low self-esteem, perfectionism, and interpersonal difficulties such as abandonment or trust issues. During EMDR therapy, the client focuses on traumatic memories while simultaneously engaging in bilateral stimulation, such as guided eye movements. This process activates specific parts of the brain, allowing the reprocessing and integration of these memories and reducing their emotional charge.   • Psychodynamic therapy focuses on how unconscious patterns, early relationships, and past experiences shape current feelings and behavior. By bringing these patterns into awareness — including recurring relationship dynamics and internal conflicts — clients can develop insight and make different choices. The therapist and client may explore transference, defenses, and recurring themes to promote lasting change in personality, mood, and interpersonal functioning. This approach is useful for understanding long-standing emotional difficulties and relational issues.
  • IFS is a parts-based model that views the mind as made up of multiple sub-personalities or "parts" (e.g., protectors, exiles) and a core Self that is calm, curious, and compassionate. Therapy helps clients recognize and work with these parts, unburden wounded or extreme parts, and restore leadership to the Self. IFS is gentle, highly experiential, and effective for trauma, inner conflict, and improving self-leadership and integration.
  • ACT assumes that psychological suffering is often caused by trying to control or avoid unpleasant internal experiences, such as anxiety or sadness. Instead of trying to eliminate these experiences, ACT teaches mindfulness and acceptance strategies to help us observe our thoughts and feelings without being overwhelmed by them. The therapy also emphasizes commitment to value-driven actions, guiding us to take steps toward what truly matters to us, despite any challenges we may face.
  • At the heart of Schema therapy is the concept of "schemas", which are deep-rooted patterns of thoughts, emotions, and behaviors that develop in response to unmet core emotional needs during childhood. These core emotional needs include things like safety, love, connection, autonomy, and self-esteem. When these needs are not properly met, maladaptive schemas develop, leading to unhealthy coping mechanisms and distress in adulthood. Through Schema therapy, we learn to identify and understand these schemas, recognize how they impact our current life, and work to change the associated thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. The goal is to meet those core emotional needs in healthier ways, leading to improved mental health and well-being. Schema therapy is particularly effective for individuals with long-lasting emotional and interpersonal difficulties.
  • EFT is a structured, evidence-based approach for couples that focuses on identifying and reshaping negative interaction patterns rooted in attachment needs. By accessing and expressing underlying emotions and needs, partners learn to respond to each other with empathy and accessibility, strengthening the emotional bond. EFT is effective for relationship distress, increasing security and intimacy.
  • Somatic approaches integrate body awareness and movement with psychological work, recognizing that trauma and stress are often stored in the body. Techniques may include breath work, grounding, sensation tracking, and gentle movement to release tension and restore regulation. These methods are helpful for trauma recovery, anxiety, chronic stress, and reconnecting mind and body for a greater sense of safety.
  • Gestalt therapy emphasizes present-moment awareness and the client's experience in the here-and-now. Through experiments, role-plays, and dialogue, clients explore unfinished business, how they make contact with others, and patterns of avoidance. The approach fosters personal responsibility, creative self-support, and greater emotional clarity and embodiment.
